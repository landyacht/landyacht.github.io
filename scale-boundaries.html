Scale Boundaries

I'd like to share with you something that popped into my head on a long drive recently.

Have you ever gotten to a point in a particular subject where you feel you understand the topic from top to bottom? That's not to say you necessarily understand everything in between the top and bottom, but you've at least reached some sort of lower boundary at which delving further would not serve your understanding.

For example, perhaps you are a computer programmer who recently learned all about operating systems and how they provide abstraction over the hardware, and you have a rough understand of what purpose the hardware serves and how it is typically organized. Would it benefit you at this point to learn electrical engineering in order to understand how exactly the components work internally? Probably not. You have reached what I call a scale boundary. Is there already some other term for this? Probably. Please let me know.

To have a truly from-the-fundamentals understanding of any subject would require an immense and impractical amount of knowledge. One does not study economics via the lens of particle physics, for instance. Unless one is deranged. Which is possible.

No, we make distinctions between different fields of study for this reason. It's impractical to learn the workings of something down to the very smallest scale. Of course, scale is fairly continuous (although I don't know of anyone studying half an atom), so we need some sort of delineation between different fields of study. These delineations are scale boundaries.

Many current scale boundaries are probably more rooted in culture and history than in any sort of objective analysis, but I think we can come up with a better formulation for scale boundaries. I won't try to say what is and is not a scale boundary; rather, I will provide a way to measure the degree of boundary-ness of any given point along the spectrum of scale. And here it is:

The strength, S, of a scale boundary, σ, is the percentage of behaviors of "things" of σ or greater scale which may be accurately predicted using theories which reference only other things of σ or greater scale.

I am not entirely happy with this formulation. Below is a list of concerns and questions. This is where you, dear reader, come in. I'd like to hear your thoughts on this matter. Ping me in #off-topic on the Programming Discord or shoot me a DM @Rogue#2017. I'd give you my email but quite frankly I don't want it cluttered up by the unwashed masses. As if masses of people are going to read this steaming line of bullshit. But I digress.

  • A "thing" is rather vague and is itself defined using some sort of scale boundary, most likely. No one defines a human in terms of quarks, for example.
  • What exactly is a "behavior" and isn't it subjective? And, again, probably defined with an existing scale boundary, since it's a property of those vague "things"?
  • What is a reasonable threshold for considering something a good scale boundary? That is, what is the scale boundary boundary? Ninety-five per cent (S = 95)? Ninety-eight (S = 98)?
  • Are there cases where a scale boundary might be stronger if we put an upper limit on scale as well? That is, are there cases when something even larger in scale suddenly requires smaller-scale theories to explain, whereas something closer to the boundary does not? For example, a knowledge aerodynamics is needed to build an aircraft, but not to build the seat inside said aircraft. This more complex formulation would have required me to write too many words too carefully, so I'll leave it as an exercise for the ambitious reader.
