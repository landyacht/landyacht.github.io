Considering the Age of Consent

Summary

I'm aware that the age of consent is a spicy topic, but I don't think what I say herein is terribly controversial. Regardless, I know people have strong opinions on this, so I think a summary might help alleviate any premature outrage.

When I say the age of consent, I mean the age at which we consider a human being to be a legal person with the liberty to enter into contracts—explicit and implicit—on their own. I consider the age of consent to sexual intercourse to be merely one instance of this broader issue; that categorization is justified below. My thesis is that age of consent is primarily an issue of psychology which differs according to societal-cultural conditions rather than one of biology or universal morality which would be constant across cultural boundaries. In specific, I argue that the age of consent is in fact too low in many parts of the United States, though it may be too high in others.

Ethical & Legal Foundations

My perspective rests on the Lockean concept of self-ownership and the resulting right of persons to enter into mutually-consentual contract. The child, or minor, appears to be a special case or exception in this system of thought, but I will show that reasonable conclusions can be derived from individualist principles without creating any contradictions. In most societies, younger members cannot enter into contract without the consent of a parent or guardian. We define "minor" for our purposes as someone to whom this restriction applies due to their age. If it were not due to age, it would more aptly be called slavery or institutionalization. However, in most societies, minors do retain other liberties even if they cannot enter into contract; for example, child abuse is illegal in the civilized world.

I affirm that these conclusions are both good for society and at least approximately correct. The exact laws may be somewhat off the mark, but it is correct to say that minors are—and ought to be—neither considered full legal persons nor mere animals. Let us work toward these conclusions from the basic principles of individual liberty.

Naïvely, one might assume the libertarian position to be "give all humans the same liberties, adult or not!" Though an admirable position, it errs by misconstruing consent. I ask: can a rock give consent? A fish? The reasonable answers are "no" and "no." Why cannot the rock give consent? It lacks the mental faculties to even conceive of consent. Why cannot the fish give consent? For the same reason. The reader may object that fish repopulate, so clearly they give consent to reproduce. This is a logical error because fish reproduce by animal instinct, not the relatively more "refined" human courtship ritual; they lack the mental faculty to conceive of consent just the same as the rock.

But what of more intelligent animals such as lions? In their species there is a courtship ritual and even an analog of marriage.¹ If adult lions, being less intelligent still than human children, can apparently give consent, why not allow children to enter into contract as soon as their intelligence rivals that of an adult lion? The answer is that there are different sorts of intelligence and that human psychology is categorically different from lion psychology. Therefore, it is wrong to say mature lions give the same category of consent as mature humans do. Lion consent is a mere analog of human consent. When we think of comparing the intelligences of animals, we generally use problem-solving ability (perhaps because this will always put us at the top?), but emotional intelligence is just as important. In fact, when it comes to consenting to sex, emotional intelligence is arguably more important.

When we speak of consent in the context of law, we speak implicitly of informed, human consent. This is important not only for deciding the place of minors in our legal system but also for invalidating so-called "contracts" created due to one or more parties being misled or lied to. Lions make their society work with lion consent, but we are not lions and there is no sense in building our human society around lion categories.

With this established, we begin to see why minors are barred from giving consent on their own. Their education, in the broadest and not necessarily academic sense of the word, is incomplete, and therefore they are unable to reach the bar of informed, human consent. For this reason, we bestow the right to consent on a minor's behalf to his or her legal guardian. On its own, this doctrine would be problematic, so I say also that a minor still retains the liberty to deny consent. Therefore, a guardian cannot simply give his or herself consent, on a minor's behalf, to do harmful things to that minor. The guardian could, technically speaking, give that consent, but the minor may revoke it at will, and the revocation takes precedence, making this technicality de facto irrelevant.

We say also that the guardian is the minor's agent of consent-receiving, since being unable to give informed consent, surely the minor is similary unable to determine whether he or she has received informed consent from another person. Because the guardian is the minor's agent of consent, the guardian becomes responsible also for aggressions committed by the minor. This is so because all interactions with other persons are either contracts, legitimized by mutual consent, or aggressions. I have just said that these two things are categorically different, but it is one that defines the other. That is, one may determine the set of aggressions by subtracting the set of consentual interactions from the set of all interactions. Therefore, the one who determines which interactions are consentual also determines, in doing so, which ones are not.

Because the guardian is responsible for the minor's actual and potential aggressions, he or she must have the right to do what is necessary to prevent such aggressions. From this fact stems the legitimacy of the various activities we call parenting, such as forbidding and punishing misbehavior, teaching manners, teaching the law, etc. The line between parenting and abuse must be drawn on a case-by-case basis using the question of whether the action is necessary to prevent the minor from aggressing against others. Naturally there will be disagreement on specifics, but we must agree at least on the procedure of inquiry.

Up to now it has been established that minors should not have the full liberties of a legal person. However, I have also posited that minors have more liberties than mere inanimate objects. Why? Minors are still human beings, containing within them the seed which will eventually blossom into a mature adult worthy of and entitled to the liberties of a legal person. To argue otherwise, we would necessarily conclude that it is acceptable to treat minors as natural resources and kill them all. This is patently absurd and contrary to humanity and the survival of our species, therefore I say that minors own themselves just the same as legal persons. However, due to their immaturity, their interactions with others must be mediated by a guardian. In this sense, the guardian provides a service to the minor who otherwise would simply be disallowed from entering any contract, being unable to give informed consent.

Finally, I will establish why consent to sex can be treated as merely an instance of consent in general. Axiomatically speaking, the argument is done as soon as we call it consent to sex. However, I want to address the objection that minors become mature enough to consent to different things at different times. For example, most people agree that minors are ready to have a summer job before they are ready to have a sexual relationship. While I don't necessarily dispute that assertion, I believe drawing lines in the sand would quickly become difficult. For example, prostitution and acting in pornography are jobs. If those are too easily categorized, what about solo pornography? Softcore pornography? Suggestive dancing? Dancing that is not intended to be suggestive but nevertheless could be? Beauty pageants? Attempting to define subcategories of consent is an endeavor that would create more headache than progress.

Issues Today & The Path Forward

The issue now is to decide the metrics and criteria we use to determine whether a minor should become a legal person or remain a minor longer. Being a resident of the United States, I will talk primarily about conditions in the US, but I consider my conclusions applicable across the world. The first error is that we have a separate age of consent for sexual relationships, determined by each state. In some states, the conditional ability to consent to sex is as low as 11. In the plurality of states, it is 16. However, legal personhood in all states is granted only at 18 or older (called the "age of majority"). My first suggestion is to unify the age of consent and age of majority.

Secondly, I believe that in many regions of my country, 18 is too low. Many people are simply not mature enough to be released from minorhood when they reach 18. Remember, for most people, their 18th birthday is before or during their senior year of high school. In more developed regions, the majority of high schoolers, seniors or otherwise, have not gained the life experience needed become independent and to understand the contracts into which they might enter.

Thirdly, I also believe that in some regions of my country and in other parts of the world, 18 may be too high. Here is the core of my argument: while biological sexual maturity is obtained quite early in life, there are good reasons why reproduction becomes more delayed as societies develop. Maslow was correct in asserting that humans have a hierarchy of needs. His exact hierarchy may have flaws, but the principle is correct. The most important needs are those which ensure the continuation of the species, namely survival and reproduction. As those needs become easier to satisfy, we may look toward other, "higher" goals, namely emotional health. When it was necessary to have many children as soon as possible due to high infant mortality and low adult life expectancy, the emotional health of those just coming out of puberty was secondary to basic survival. As such practices become less necessary, the emotional and social benefits of waiting for greater maturity and being more selective in sexual partners come to the fore.

Because diet, food security, healthcare, health habits, environmental conditions, and the expectations placed on the young vary across regions and cultures, it would be harmful to attempt to standarize the age of legal personhood across a country as vast as the United States, or even across smaller countries. In some rural areas, children take on responsibilities and learn to conduct themselves maturely at a much earlier age than in some urban or suburban areas. My aim here is not to prescribe a particular way of parenting that I believe to be the best compromise. Instead, I believe decisions should be made more locally based on the culture of the area. Raising or lowering the age of consent cannot be called good or bad in isolation; rather, it is good when the threshold moves toward where it ought to be for a particular community, and bad when it begins to deviate in either direction.
